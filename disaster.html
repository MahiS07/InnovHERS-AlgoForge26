Learn how satellites in space help us spot floods, fires, earthquakes, and storms before they cause widespread damage.

Disaster Types

Floods

Satellites take pictures of Earth from space. When floods happen, water covers land that was dry before. Satellites can "see" this water because it reflects light differently than land or plants.

Think of it like comparing two photos of your neighborhood — one before rain and one after. The satellite does the same thing, but from hundreds of kilometers above Earth.

Special cameras see both visible light (like our eyes) and invisible infrared light
Radar sensors can see through clouds and work at night or during storms
Comparison of before and after images shows exactly where water has spread

Satellites like Sentinel-1 use C-band radar at 5.405 GHz frequency. Water appears smooth and dark because it reflects radar waves away from the sensor. Flooded vegetation creates a unique backscatter signature due to double-bounce reflection between water surface and plant stems.

Landsat and Sentinel-2 use NDWI formula: (Green - NIR) / (Green + NIR). Values above 0.3 typically indicate water presence.

When satellites spot floods early, governments can warn people to evacuate and move to safety. This early warning can save thousands of lives.

Imagine knowing a flood is coming to your town 6-24 hours before it arrives. That's enough time to move people, animals, and important belongings to higher ground.

Early warnings give people time to evacuate safely
Live maps show which roads are blocked and where help is needed
Damage reports help governments plan rebuilding efforts

Use the slider on the right to compare images. Move it left and right to see how the landscape changes when water floods the area.

What to look for in the images:

Dark or blue areas are where water has covered the ground
Buildings and roads appear as straight lines and shapes
Green areas are plants and trees, which turn dark when underwater

Wildfires

Before
After

Satellites have special heat-sensing cameras that detect hot spots on Earth's surface. When forests catch fire, they become very hot and satellites can detect this heat from space.

It's like a thermal camera you might have seen that shows warm things in red and orange colors. Satellites scan Earth twice a day looking for these hot spots.

Heat detection can spot fires as small as a basketball court
Daily scanning checks the entire planet twice every 24 hours
Smoke tracking shows which direction the fire is spreading

Fire radiative power (FRP) measures fire intensity in megawatts. Higher FRP indicates more intense combustion. Satellites calculate FRP using brightness temperature difference between mid-IR (4μm) and thermal-IR (11μm) channels.

Burned area mapping uses Normalized Burn Ratio (NBR): (NIR - SWIR) / (NIR + SWIR). Comparing pre and post-fire NBR reveals burn severity.

When satellites spot a wildfire, they send alerts to firefighters within hours. This helps fire crews know where to send resources and which communities need to evacuate.

Wildfires can spread very fast, especially on windy days. Satellite data helps predict where the fire will go next, giving people time to escape safely.

Fire prediction shows where flames might spread next
Evacuation alerts warn nearby towns to leave safely
Air quality monitoring protects people from harmful smoke

Move the slider to see how the forest changes. Green forests turn black or dark brown after burning.

What to look for in fire images:

Red/orange spots show active flames still burning
Black areas are recently burned land with no plants left
Gray clouds are smoke showing which way the fire is moving

Earthquakes

Satellites send radar signals to Earth and measure how the ground moves. After an earthquake, the ground shifts — sometimes by just a few centimeters, sometimes by meters. Satellites can detect these tiny movements.

Think of it like taking two photos of your room from the exact same spot. If something moved between photos, you'd notice. Satellites do this with radar to see if the ground moved.

Radar comparison checks the same area every 6 days
Precise measurements detect ground shifts as small as 1 inch
Damage maps show which buildings collapsed or were damaged

InSAR creates interferograms by calculating phase differences between radar acquisitions. Each color fringe represents 2.8cm of ground movement. Coherence loss indicates severe ground disruption or building collapse.

After an earthquake, rescue teams need to know which areas were hit hardest. Satellite images show them where buildings collapsed and which roads are blocked, helping them reach victims faster.

Within 12-24 hours of an earthquake, satellites provide detailed maps showing damage across entire cities. This helps organize rescue efforts and medical supplies.

Quick assessment within one day of the earthquake
Priority zones guide rescue teams to hardest-hit areas
Utility analysis shows which power lines and water pipes broke

Compare the images to see earthquake damage. Buildings change shape or disappear. Ground cracks appear as dark lines.

What to look for:

Cracks in ground appear as straight dark lines across the land
Landslides on hills and mountains triggered by shaking
Sunken areas where ground liquefied and sank down

Cyclones

Before
After

Special satellites stay positioned above one spot on Earth 24/7, watching for storms. They take pictures every 15 minutes to track how fast the storm is spinning and growing.

These satellites measure cloud temperature (colder clouds mean stronger storms) and wind speed to determine if a hurricane is Category 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5.

Every 15 minutes new images show storm's movement and growth
See through clouds using special cameras to find the eye
Wind measurements show how fast the storm is spinning

Dvorak technique analyzes cloud patterns to estimate intensity. Central Dense Overcast (CDO) temperature below -70°C indicates strong convection. Eye diameter inversely correlates with wind speed.

Satellites predict where and when the storm will hit land, giving people 2-5 days to evacuate safely. This early warning saves thousands of lives.

By tracking the storm's path, officials know which cities need to evacuate and where to send emergency supplies.

2-5 days warning before the storm reaches land
Flood predictions show which coastal areas will be underwater
Rainfall forecasts help prepare for flooding even far from coast

Look at the spiral shape. Tighter spirals mean faster spinning. A clear circular hole in the center (the "eye") means the storm is very strong.

What to look for:

The eye is the calm center where skies are clear
Spiral arms bring wind and rain as they rotate
Bright white clouds at the top mean very cold temperatures = strong storm

Landslides

Satellites watch mountainsides and hills, looking for changes. When a landslide happens, green forest suddenly turns brown as trees are swept away, exposing dirt and rocks.

Some satellites can even detect tiny movements in the ground (a few millimeters per month) before a landslide happens, giving early warnings.

Vegetation loss shows where trees were swept away
Ground movement detected before slides happen
Risk zones identified by analyzing slope steepness

Susceptibility mapping combines slope angle, geology, rainfall, and vegetation data. Slopes >30° with sparse vegetation after heavy rain show highest risk. Persistent scatterer InSAR tracks mm/year creep that precedes failures.

After heavy rain, satellites quickly map new landslides to find blocked roads and identify areas at risk for more slides.

Sometimes landslides block rivers, creating temporary dams. If these dams break, they cause massive floods downstream. Satellites help monitor these dangerous situations.

Blocked roads found so emergency crews can plan new routes
River blockages monitored to prevent deadly downstream floods
High-risk areas watched closely after first landslides

Look for brown streaks down the mountainside against green forest. The top of the landslide looks like a horseshoe shape where the ground broke away.

What to look for:

Scar at the top where the ground first broke loose
Flow path shows the track of rocks and mud sliding down
Pile at bottom where debris spreads out and stops

Reading Satellite Images

Color Changes

Water looks darker or blue, fires show bright red or orange. Green areas are forests, brown/gray are cities or bare ground.

Texture Changes

Smooth surfaces become rough and broken after earthquakes. Buildings that collapsed look messy and unorganized.

Shape Changes

Coastlines move inland during floods. Rivers become wider. Forests have new burned-out gaps after fires.

Heat Signatures

Special heat-sensing cameras see fires even through thick smoke. Bright spots show where fires are actively burning.

Cloud Patterns

Spiral swirls are cyclones or hurricanes. Dense white clusters mean heavy rain and storm systems approaching.
